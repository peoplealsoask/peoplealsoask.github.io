How Many GB Do 10,000 Photos Take Up? A Complete Guide to Photo Storage Sizes in 2026

Wondering how many GB 10,000 photos need? We break down storage sizes for JPEG, RAW, HEIC, and more. Plus tips to save space.

Executive Summary: The storage space for 10,000 photos ranges from about 15 GB (small, compressed JPEGs) to over 500 GB (high-resolution RAW files). For typical smartphone photos (12MP JPEG), expect ~30-40 GB.

If you’ve ever wondered how many GB 10,000 photos take up, the answer isn’t one-size-fits-all. It depends on resolution, file format, compression, and even the camera you use. Here’s the high-level breakdown: for standard smartphone images (12 megapixels, JPEG), 10,000 photos consume about 30–40 GB. For high-end DSLR RAW files, that number can skyrocket to 500+ GB. Let’s dig into the numbers so you can plan your storage wisely.

# What Determines Photo File Size?

Before we crunch the numbers, understand the key factors:

  • Resolution (megapixels): Higher resolution = larger file.
  • File format: RAW > TIFF > JPEG > HEIC (generally).
  • Compression: JPEG quality settings dramatically affect size.
  • Subject complexity: Detailed scenes produce larger files.
Photo Type Average Size per Photo Storage for 10,000 Photos
Low-res web JPEG (0.3 MP) 50 KB 0.5 GB
Standard smartphone JPEG (12 MP) 3–4 MB 30–40 GB
High-quality DSLR JPEG (24 MP) 8–12 MB 80–120 GB
RAW (24 MP) 30–50 MB 300–500 GB
HEIC (iPhone, 12 MP) 1.5–2.5 MB 15–25 GB

# Real-World Scenarios

# 1. Smartphone Users (12 MP JPEG, ~3.5 MB each)

Most modern phones take photos around 12 MP. With 10,000 photos, you’re looking at 35 GB. iPhones using HEIC can halve that.

# 2. Professional Photographers (24 MP RAW, ~40 MB each)

For a wedding or event shoot, 10,000 RAW images could eat up 400 GB. That’s why pros carry multiple drives.

# 3. Social Media & Web (Compressed JPEGs, ~1 MB each)

If you’ve downloaded images from social media or use low-res settings, 10,000 files might be just 10 GB.

# How to Calculate Your Own Storage Needs

  1. Check a sample photo’s file size (right-click > Properties/Info).
  2. Multiply by 10,000.
  3. Add a buffer for future shots.

For example, if your photos average 5 MB, you need 50 GB. For safety, buy a device with at least double that capacity.

# Tips to Reduce Photo Storage

  • Use HEIC/HEIF format (saves 50% space vs JPEG).
  • Compress JPEGs for non-critical backups.
  • Store RAW files selectively – keep only the best shots.
  • Cloud storage with automatic optimization (e.g., Google Photos “Storage saver”).

# Frequently Asked Questions

# How many photos can 1 GB hold?

It varies: about 200–300 smartphone JPEGs, 500–600 HEIC images, or 20–30 RAW files per GB.

# Do videos take up more space than photos?

Yes. A 1-minute 4K video can be 400 MB – equivalent to 100–150 photos.

# What’s the best way to store 10,000 photos?

A combination of an external SSD (e.g., 500 GB) and cloud backup. For recommendations, check out the best external hard drives for photos .

# Why does my photo library show more storage than expected?

Likely due to duplicate files, thumbnails, and iCloud/Google Photos caches. Use cleaning tools to reclaim space.

# Will AI image compression reduce sizes in 2026?

Yes, emerging formats like JPEG XL and AVIF offer better compression, potentially cutting file sizes by 30–50% without quality loss.

# Conclusion

To answer “How many GB do 10,000 photos take up?”: For most people, 30–40 GB. But always check your own images. As storage gets cheaper and compression improves, managing large libraries becomes easier. Plan ahead, and your memories will stay safe without breaking the bank.
